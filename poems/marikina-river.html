Marikina River

by Hanyi

They say the river rose like a throat clearing, and did not stop.
The girls sang dusk to sleep
folded socks like small prayers
tucked letters into the hush of dark.
Somewhere,
a lantern was clicked off—
its glow mistaken
for the last they’d need.
Now— a sock in a hedge, a toothbrush in the mud, a name tag peeled from its lanyard by water.
The news gave it a name. The trees answered with silence. I call it the ache
of not knowing what became of the girls who braided ribbons into one another’s hair that morning.
A leaf circles the drain,
caught in the current,
as if nothing ever happened.
I walk with water in my ears. Even the cicadas pause.
The wind lifts a street sign. It almost sounds like a voice.
