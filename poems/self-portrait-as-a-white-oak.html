Self-Portrait as a White Oak

by Hanyi

The morning quiets—
the ground beneath me hums,
sugar-warm, stitched into a crown
of moss. I press my body until it forgets
the sky, until it forgets I am not rooted.
Some days, this stillness feels like the breath
of the world I am learning to follow. But stillness
stirs—by afternoon, the wind begins in my chest. Hope threads through
my ribs, out my mouth, gliding along
my skin, peeling it
like sycamore bark gone smooth. The bruises
spring into bloom. I release
each wood flake the wind leaves behind, never
knowing the rings have already circled.
The roots unspool into names
I never meant to carry, branches quivering
as they threaten to flower. Before
the wind drains out of my chest, I echo
into seeds scattering from the highways
to the bridges. My heart flutters
like a moth, mistaking light for safety.
Roots sip from the dark because they must. I survive silently
until my name blooms, blisters, then drops.
I’d learn the patience of white oaks—how
to wait without breaking, to let the seasons
carve through me without mourning
leaves they leave behind. Instead, I am
here—a tangle of nerve endings.
Stilled, but never still.
