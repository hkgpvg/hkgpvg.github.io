Nǎinai

by Hanyi

There’s a moment each time I set
the water to boil, when I hear
the low hum you stretched over years
as you read to yourself, words cotton-
quiet as you mouthed them, before
I remember. The kettle speaks the same low note
sometimes. The house was louder, then—
it buckled under your laughter, how it bristled
each time your kitchen chair shrieked back
from the table & you clattered Mama’s tea
pots into the sink. Last week, I found your words—
handwritten, steam-blotted—inside the spine
of your cookbook, in the margins of your recipe
for tomato-egg stir fry: more time, it should feel like it will burn
but I promise it won’t. It never does. The page has gone
feather-soft in my hands. It’s peaceful, in a way, once
it gives itself over to time—the page has worked
& worked. At dusk, you settle into the radiator. The maples
drop their seeds each time you click. I always thought
I’d stop hearing our porch step’s faithful groan,
thinking you’d returned from the library, fresh
cookbooks under your arms. Or from mah-jong, your fingers
still feeling for the tiles. But
I haven’t—this morning, when I heard it, I opened
the door. The cold was honest, wide. It tasted
like the alphabet as I’d learned it, hopeful
in your shadow, each consonant a little strange
yet certain as I spoke.
