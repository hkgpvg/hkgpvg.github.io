quiet storms & dried plums

by Hanyi

nǎinai once said
the steam from pu-erh carries memory. I lift
the chipped cup, watch the fog curl
like ghost-breath in the absence between us.
on the narrow street bordering the yard,
a child kneels at the frayed edge
of a bamboo mat, still streaked with afternoon
light. her mother threads dusk through
her tangled braids, fingers snagging
knots, weaving patience into each strand.
through crinkling plastic, her small hands
dig for the last dried plum—syrup-sticky, running
down her wrist. umbrellas fracture into neon
shadows against the wet concrete, shop signs
flickering in the rain.
each footstep softly echoes, leaving
a second body behind: the clinking
chipped cup ghosting between nǎinai’s palms.
this cracked cup, these cracked hands, her cracked lips:
even now, she swallows the sweetness that remains.
by the rusted gate, its path
worn into the asphalt, petals fold vermillion
into the wind. that winter, i saw a plum blossom
erupt from the snow. i swear—it couldn’t have
& shouldn’t have, not in this cold. but it did.
