The World I Knew

by Hanyi

The slide still collects rain. At the bottom, a shallow pool gathers
maple leaves, sun splintering into
shards of shadow & light. When we were children, we called it stew. Each afternoon, we dared each other to drink it.
Now, it glints in the sun, a fresh cut stinging
in sticky sweat. Gravel grips to the smell
of hot rubber, August heat. It scraped
our knees raw, took refuge in our damp socks, no matter what
shoes we wore. We never sat still
long enough to care. We declared
the floor was lava and leapt the faded, chalky seafoam tiles, every jump a wager
from swing base to seesaw. We bet White Rabbit candies on it, laughter echoing off the monkey bars, no matter who won.
We found a butterfly, wings torn.
We dug a grave to fill with daisies. We offered three seconds of silence.
Then, someone yelled “tag!” and we forgot. The butterfly still
seeps into the soil.
The bars by the jungle gym still trap warmth in the afternoon but no one hangs upside-down anymore.
The caution sign has changed: no climbing. The gate creaks the same one syllable. On my way, I hear it
ebb and flow into sound. The slide
still glistens with rain. A beetle
scrambles through the gravel, almost finding
my shadow with its shadow as I count cracks
working their way through the sidewalk, just to see
if this path still remembers my weight.
