Field Elegy at the Edge of the World

by Hanyi

Wheat, sprouting from the suitcase laying
unopened, unpacked in my room. Wheat, coarse
and yellow, dirt-flecked. Stalks of it
rising from the zipper’s teeth, crawling over
the juniper wood floors.
The suitcase in my room sprouted
wheat –– coarse, yellow, dirty
wheat. Stalks rose from the zipper’s teeth,
rugged blades whispering the names
my grandparents gave up when they were sent
West. Their schoolbooks confiscated,
futures folded into train tickets
that rattled like thin bones.
I touch the suitcase and it
shivers, opens it mouth to spit out red dust
from a province I have never seen
but somehow breathe like a second
language.
Out steps my grandfather, a half-formed
shadow that arrives before his body,
unsure if he deserves to take up space.
His hands are cracked from planting
dreams that did not belong to him,
from burying questions he could not ask.
I reach for them, and their bodies dissolve
into grain––futures ground down to feed a nation
that told them to swallow their
own hunger.
The suitcase jolts, wheat
pouring out of its seams and covering the floor.
I kneel, trying to gather every stalk,
but they slip through my hands.
