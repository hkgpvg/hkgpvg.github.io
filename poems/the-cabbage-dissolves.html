The Cabbage Dissolves

by Hanyi

Tonight, I push open the sun-room window. The Pudong air
tastes metallic, like the clouds holding their breath
above the city, unsure whether to bless us with rain
or rust.
Steam from Mama’s pot of cabbage-egg stew bruises
the sky grey-green, a lone cabbage leaf curling on my plate, cooling in the draft
that slips between us.
My grandmother says the same wind once howled
through cracked windows wide as open mouths.
My grandfather scraped mud from roots
and called it a new recipe for soup. We chew
the memories our family holds in our pale veins. In the bowl, we sip whispers of how we crouched
behind market stalls, waiting for blemished vegetables
to sweep into our palms. Yet the refrigerator swells
with excess: plastic towers humming
with condensation, stacked bowls softening
as rice hardens into brittle bones. Still, we rinse
spoiled greens, cut around the rot and wash away
the dirt, as if it can be forgiven and forgotten. I open
the door, inhaling the cold, sour air of a second chance
as molting cabbage heads sleep beside me.
My grandmother wipes the counter again, squinting
as my grandfather picks raindrops of sesame from his plate,
carefully, like they’ll dissolve into the soil otherwise. And maybe
they will—outside, the evening willing us
toward forgetting. I chew. We chew. The cabbage dissolves
into silence.
