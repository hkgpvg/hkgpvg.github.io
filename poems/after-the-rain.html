After the Rain

by Hanyi

I return to the woods after the rain, the slope
slick with crushed beetles and the kind of quiet
the ferns keep after bending too far towards the sun, forgetting
our names. Once, we dragged fallen branches from the brush, leaned
pine against pine, built a roof of leaves and stubbornness
and called it shelter. We crouched together, shoulder
to shoulder, the crevices of our palms dirt-streaked, bracing
as the walls leaned their whole trembling weight into us.
Now, the ground tilts differently. My feet slip as the earth loosens from the topography of my own remembering. Typhoons have rehearsed its erasure, stripped
the ground of its soil, stitched silence into its bark.
Only twigs remain, half-buried, awkwardly threading
stitches where they don’t belong. I stand too tall now, branches scratching my shoulders, violent strangers. Somewhere, you walk
streets stacked with glass and concrete, your breath fogging
the face of another sky. There, the air carries wet moss
in its scent, the hollow ache of unfinished adventures. I search
for your footprints in the softening earth––but the rain swallows them
before my mouth can take the shape of your name.
