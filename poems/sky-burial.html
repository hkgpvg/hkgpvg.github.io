Sky Burial

by Hanyi

From the voice of the rogyapa—the one who prepares the body for offering.
I.
I wake before the frost lifts from the slate, the kettle hissing—steam climbing the dark,
stalling in the thin air. The valley sleeps under its blue shawl. My blade rests where I left it, its oiled edge catching what early light slips from the prayer wheel.
They say not to name the dead. That a name is a rope snagged on stone,
tethering them too close to the land. So I don’t. In her face, I find echoes of the girl from the mountain school above the valley, who braided her hair with red string.
I gather her life into her chuba, still warm from the hearth.
I knot the braided sash, silver clip still in the pocket.
She never had time for laces, felt boots thudding
down the wooden steps.
Outside, her cousin fingers his prayer beads,
pacing along the stone-stacked mani wall.
བཟང་རྒུའི་གོས་རྒྱན་སྤྲས་ཀྱང་སྙིང་པོ་ཅི།།
What meaning in wrapping it in silk?
II.
The offering arrives wrapped in saffron cloth. Her hands still ink-stained from schoolbooks. The monks have said their prayers. The juniper smolders. I begin, the silence thicker than flesh.
I gather her life into a ball of tsampa—
dense with ghee, the kind she pressed
into shape with her flour-dusted hands.
I dip these memories into butter tea, steam
unfurling like prayer flags in the wind.
The tea is salty, thick on my tongue.
Her fingers shone with oil, flecked with barley.
Outside, a goat coughs by the tether post.
ཞིམ་རྒུའི་ཁ་ཟས་ཟོས་ཀྱང་སྙིང་པོ་ཅི།།
What meaning in feeding it delicacies?
III.
The vultures wait—
wing folded tight against their ribs
heads tilted, measuring the ground.
བཅོམ་ལྡན་འདས་ཤཱཀྱ་ཐུབ་པ
A song keeps measure in my head, one I don’t remember learning. Breath sets to breath.
Hands move when they should—
nothing hurried, nothing wasted.
I step back.
The weight is no longer mine.
I gather her life into the compact
tucked in the sleeve of her chuba, just a dab
of barley powder on her cheeks. She would blot the color once,
a soft shadow on the white of her khata.
She purses her lips in the mirror’s oval,
a fingertip smoothing the bow. At the low table, a butter lamp burns.
མཚར་རྒུའི་མཛེས་བཟོ་བྱས་ཀྱང་སྙིང་པོ་ཅི།། What meaning in rouge, in perfume?
IV.
The sky ashens beneath turning wings. The vultures have eaten. The soul, as they say, has flown.
And I walk back through cairns and prayer flags, the same path
I will take
when it’s my turn
to offer my bo
