No One Knew the City Could Burn This Quietly

by Hanyi

Hong Kong, 2020

Somewhere, a metro station entrance flamed shut.
Neon shop signs flicker behind the gauze of white haze.
Spray-painted slogans curl from the walls, half-
erased by ash, by rain. Luggage wheels scythed
the sidewalk, teeth grinding molars into the street.
The city's silence rattled the bones of the curbs.
We walked.

Umbrellas bud like orchids before bloom, rain
shaken from their ribs. Beneath, swells of masked faces.
Across Hennessy Road, a spine of bricks traced a path
no one dared follow—laid like a warning, an offering
of bones no one would bury. In the crosswalk, a backpack
knelt, its straps scorched, limp. No one touched it.
We walked.

A molotov cocktail hissed from a trash bin
with a crackling voice; familiar, yet something I couldn't name.
My father's voice cracked open: "Bié kàn."
My mother whispered, her mouth barely moving: "English."
Smoke threaded into the cotton of my shirt.
My brother's hand in mine, grief misting our skin. Children, still
too young for the language. Too quiet for the grief.
We walked.

Somewhere, a man burned clean through
my mother's iPad, scorching the kitchen table
with silence. It was the kind of silence that hollows
a house from the inside-out. All morning, I mouthed
the word for "home," but no language came—only
the question of what burned first: the man, or the name
of the place that once felt prayer-natural in my mouth.
We walked. And as we walked, I counted the echoes
of our footsteps—and the ones we didn't hear
anymore. Inside someone's pocket, a phone sang.
No one answered. It settled. We crossed
a bridge. We crossed a barricade. Our wheels dragged
into a kind of song. And the city dragged
behind us.
